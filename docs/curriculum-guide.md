# How the curriculum mapping works

Every clinical page on this site carries a **curriculum mapping** box. This page explains what the numbers mean and links to the source document on the ISCP website.

## The source document

All curriculum references currently on this site are to the **ISCP Trauma & Orthopaedic Surgery Curriculum**:

**[T&O Surgery Curriculum, Version 3, August 2026](https://www.iscp.ac.uk/media/1543/to-surgery-curriculum-aug-2026-version-3-updated-17jun2026.pdf)** (PDF, hosted by ISCP)

A neurosurgical mapping layer is planned but not yet built. When it is, it'll reference:

**[ISCP Neurosurgery Curriculum, Feb 2026](https://www.iscp.ac.uk/media/1432/2-updated-neurosurgery-curriculum-feb2026-clean.pdf)** (PDF, hosted by ISCP)

!!! warning "Curricula get revised, page numbers can change"
    Every page reference on this site (e.g. "p.53") refers to the page number **printed at the bottom of the curriculum PDF**, not the PDF file's internal page count, and it is not a permanent identifier. ISCP updates these documents periodically. If a citation looks wrong, check you're comparing against the same version quoted in the box, and if ISCP has published a newer version, the reference may need updating. Treat page numbers as "correct as of the version stated", not as permanently fixed.

## Reading a knowledge item: the X/Y/Z number

Most curriculum mapping boxes quote a pattern like `2/4/4`. This is always three numbers separated by slashes, and they always mean the same three training phases, in the same order:

| Position | Training phase |
|---|---|
| 1st number | **Core** (CT1-2 / ST1-2, generalist surgical training before subspecialisation) |
| 2nd number | **ST3-8** (all trainees, regardless of eventual subspecialty) |
| 3rd number | **Specialty interest** (trainees who have chosen spine as their area of focus) |

## What the numbers themselves mean

The curriculum uses two different scales depending on whether the item is a knowledge item or a hands-on skill. This site's mapping boxes are always explicit about which one applies. Here is the definition of each:

### Knowledge scale (Basic Science, Critical Conditions, Assessments, Treatments)

| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 1 | Knows of |
| 2 | Knows basic concepts |
| 3 | Knows generally |
| 4 | Knows specifically and broadly |

### Skills scale (Applied Clinical Skills)

| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 0 | No experience expected |
| 1 | Has observed, or knows of |
| 2 | Can manage with assistance |
| 3 | Can manage the whole procedure, but may need assistance |
| 4 | Able to manage without assistance, including common complications |

Skills use a 0-4 scale rather than 1-4. "0" exists specifically to mark procedures a trainee at that level isn't expected to have experienced.

So `2/4/4` means: core trainees are expected to know basic concepts, while both general ST3-8 trainees and spine-interest trainees are expected to know the topic specifically and broadly.

## A worked example

From the [Cauda Equina Syndrome](emergencies/cauda-equina-syndrome.md) page:

> Knowledge level: 3 (core) / 4 (ST3-8) / 4 (specialty interest)

This is a knowledge item, using the 1-4 scale. Even core trainees (position 1) are expected to know it *generally* (3), not just know of it, which is a noticeably higher bar than most other spine topics carry at core level. Every trainee from ST3 onwards, regardless of subspecialty, is expected to know it specifically and broadly (4). That combination is a fair proxy for how safety-critical the curriculum authors consider the topic, and it is worth noticing the core-level number being unusually high.

Compare that with a typical elective topic, from [ACDF vs Cervical Disc Replacement](cervical-spine/acdf-vs-disc-replacement.md):

> Skills: ACDF (p.66, level 0/1/4)

This is a skills item. Core trainees aren't expected to have experienced it (0). General ST3-8 trainees are expected to have observed it or know of it, but not to perform it (1). Only trainees with a declared spine special interest are expected to reach full independence, including managing complications (4). The jump straight from 1 to 4, skipping any middle ground for the general ST3-8 trainee, is itself informative: this procedure isn't something you're expected to become competent at unless spine is your chosen path.

## Assessment routes

Some mapping boxes also name how a topic is assessed within ISCP. The main ones referenced on this site:

- **CBD** — Case-Based Discussion
- **CEX** — Clinical Evaluation Exercise
- **PBA** — Procedure-Based Assessment

Critical conditions are typically assessed via CBD or CEX (they're about judgement and knowledge, not a specific procedure). Skills items are assessed via PBA when a trainee logs the relevant procedure.

## If something looks wrong

If a page number, a competency level, or a quoted curriculum line doesn't match what you find in the source PDF, that's worth flagging. Curriculum documents get revised and it's possible a citation was accurate for an earlier version and hasn't been updated. Check the version and date stated at the top of this page against the PDF you're looking at before assuming either source is wrong.
