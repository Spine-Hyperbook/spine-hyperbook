## Introduction

Cauda equina syndrome (CES) is compression of the lumbosacral nerve roots below the level of the spinal cord (typically below L1–L2), causing a characteristic combination of bladder, bowel, saddle sensory and lower limb motor/sensory disturbance. It is one of the few true orthopaedic surgical emergencies and one of the highest-volume sources of litigation in UK spinal practice.

## Aetiology

- Central lumbar disc prolapse (most common)
- Spinal stenosis with superimposed disc prolapse
- Tumour, primary or metastatic
- Epidural haematoma or abscess
- Trauma
- Iatrogenic — post-surgical haematoma or direct injury

## Clinical presentation

- Bilateral or alternating sciatica
- Saddle (perianal) sensory disturbance
- Bladder dysfunction: painless retention, loss of urge to void, or loss of urgency to defecate
- Reduced anal tone on PR examination
- Variable lower limb motor and sensory deficit, may be asymmetric

Presentations are commonly classified descriptively:

| Category | Features |
|---|---|
| CES-Suspected | Back pain with bilateral radiculopathy, no bladder/bowel symptoms yet |
| CES-Incomplete | Altered urinary sensation, loss of desire to void, or poor stream, without frank retention |
| CES-Retention (CES-R) | Painless urinary retention with overflow incontinence |
| CES-Complete | Complete, established sensory and motor loss |

## Investigation

Emergency MRI whole spine is the investigation of choice and should not be delayed for a PR examination result if MRI access is the rate-limiting step. Bladder scan for post-void residual supports staging.

## Management

Surgical decompression is indicated for CES-Incomplete, CES-R and CES-Complete.

!!! question "Controversy: does timing within 24-48h actually change outcome?"
    The evidence is genuinely mixed, this is a good exam topic precisely because it resists a clean answer.

    - Decompress as soon as feasible rather than promising patients a specific hour-count.
    - Continence status *at presentation*, not time-to-surgery, is the most consistently reported prognostic factor.
    - Document clinical reasoning explicitly, given how disproportionately this condition features in litigation.

## Medicolegal note

CES is disproportionately represented in NHS litigation claims relative to its incidence, largely due to delayed recognition in primary care and delayed MRI access rather than surgical delay once diagnosed.

## Evidence base

| Study | Key finding |
|---|---|
| Definition and surgical timing in CES: an updated systematic review, 2023 (110 studies, 52,008 patients) | Only 14.5% of included studies used an established CES definition (Fraser, BASS, or Gleave & MacFarlane criteria); timing definitions are similarly inconsistent across the literature. |
| Presentation, management and outcomes of CES up to one year, *Lancet Regional Health Europe*, 2022 (UK multi-centre prospective cohort) | Pooled systematic-review logistic regression of 322 patients found sensory/motor deficit, urinary incontinence and rectal dysfunction more likely to improve with decompression within 48h, though the paper is explicit that timing's true effect remains contested. |
| Cauda equina syndrome treated by surgical decompression: influence of timing, *European Spine Journal* | Prospective cohort of 33 patients: no significant difference in outcome between <24h, 24-48h, and >48h groups. Continence status at presentation was the dominant prognostic factor. |
