## Introduction

Cauda equina syndrome (CES) is compression of the lumbosacral nerve roots below the level of the spinal cord (typically below L1-L2), causing a characteristic combination of bladder, bowel, sexual, saddle sensory and lower limb motor/sensory disturbance. It is one of the few true orthopaedic surgical emergencies and one of the highest-volume sources of litigation in UK spinal practice. CES affects roughly 1-3 people per 100,000 population in England, with around 8,000 suspected cases assessed each year ([GIRFT](https://gettingitrightfirsttime.co.uk/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/National-Suspected-Cauda-Equina-Pathway-March-2026.pdf)).

!!! info "Most suspected CES is not actually CES"
    In the largest prospective UK cohort to look at this directly ([Hoeritzauer et al., *Neurology* 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33177221/)), 69% of patients referred with suspected CES had no radiographic evidence of cauda equina compression on MRI, and only around a quarter had confirmed compression, consistent with an earlier systematic review's estimate of roughly 19%. A related Oxford analysis has separately reported that over 80% of urgent MRIs requested for suspected CES show no compression ([SuCESS score development, *BJS* 2026](https://academic.oup.com/bjs/article/113/Supplement_2/znag018.007/8551044)). This matters for how the whole topic should be taught: the diagnostic challenge isn't just recognising CES, it's managing a high-volume pathway where true positives are a minority, without either over-scanning everyone or missing the genuine emergencies. The ongoing **[CESER study](https://oxfordbrc.nihr.ac.uk/1-2m-award-to-improve-diagnosis-of-emergency-spinal-condition/)** (Cauda Equina Syndrome Early Recognition, NIHR-funded, Oxford) is specifically trying to improve this, recruiting 2,000 patients with suspected CES to identify which symptoms actually predict a confirmed diagnosis, worth watching for results.

## Aetiology

- Central lumbar disc prolapse (most common)
- Spinal stenosis with superimposed disc prolapse
- Tumour, primary or metastatic
- Epidural haematoma or abscess
- Trauma
- Iatrogenic, post-surgical haematoma or direct injury

## Classification

There is no single internationally agreed definition of CES. A [systematic review of 110 studies, 52,008 patients (Mustafa et al., *PLOS ONE* 2023)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10159340/) found that only 14.5% used an established definition at all, the rest used study-specific criteria, most commonly built around urinary dysfunction, perianal sensory change, and reduced anal tone in varying combinations. Where a named definition was used, the [Fraser criteria](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19887225/) and the BASS definition were the most common, followed by [Gleave and MacFarlane](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8782783/).

The most widely used classification, and the one worth being fluent in, is the two-stage [Gleave and MacFarlane system](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8782783/). Worth being precise here: Gleave and MacFarlane's original classification defined only CESI and CESR. "CES-Suspected" is a later, widely used clinical addition (including in the GIRFT pathway) to capture patients before bladder involvement, not part of the original 2002 classification, worth knowing so you don't attribute it to a source that never defined it.

| Category | Features |
|---|---|
| CES-Suspected | Back pain with bilateral radiculopathy, no bladder/bowel symptoms yet |
| CES-Incomplete (CESI) | Altered urinary sensation, loss of desire to void, or poor stream, without frank retention, bladder still under executive control |
| CES-Retention (CESR) | Painless urinary retention with overflow incontinence, bladder no longer under executive control |
| CES-Complete | Complete, established sensory and motor loss |

The transition from CESI to CESR is generally considered to reflect a step-change in the degree of nerve root injury, and therefore carries prognostic weight, though the underlying pathophysiology remains debated and the point of transition can be hard to pin down precisely in practice (see the timing controversy below).

## Clinical presentation

- Bilateral or alternating sciatica
- Saddle (perianal) sensory disturbance
- Bladder dysfunction, most commonly urgency, altered sensation or a poor stream rather than frank retention at first presentation
- Sexual dysfunction: in men, this typically presents as erectile dysfunction; in women, as reduced genital sensation or anorgasmia. Both sexes can experience reduced perineal/genital sensation more generally. This is a GIRFT-recognised presenting feature and worth asking about directly, patients frequently won't volunteer it unprompted
- Variable lower limb motor and sensory deficit, may be asymmetric

## Examination findings, and how much to trust them

This is the part of CES teaching most in need of updating. Digital rectal examination (DRE) of anal tone is close to universally taught as a mandatory part of CES assessment. On the current evidence, testing anal tone specifically does not meaningfully help rule CES in or out, sensitivity is poor, one large study found no statistical association with MRI-confirmed compression at all, and the practice is retained in guidance and training mainly for documentation and medicolegal reasons (see below), not because it performs well as a diagnostic test. That distinction, between "expected practice" and "clinically useful test," is worth trainees noticing and weighing up for themselves rather than assuming the two are the same thing.

Worth being precise about which part of the PR exam this applies to: the strongest negative evidence relates specifically to resting **anal tone** (a motor finding), not to sensory testing performed during the same examination. Conflating the two risks trainees concluding the whole PR exam is worthless, which the evidence doesn't support.

| Finding | What the evidence actually shows |
|---|---|
| Anal tone (DRE) | A [systematic review and meta-analysis of 6 studies, 741 patients](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35180641/) found sensitivity ranging only 0.23-0.53 across studies, concluding DRE of anal tone "carries a high risk of false reassurance" and is "not recommended in any clinical setting." A separate [study of 1005 patients](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34796788/) found no association between abnormal anal tone and MRI-confirmed compression at all |
| Reduced anal/perianal sensation | A distinct, sensory finding, tested separately from tone during the same examination. Mixed but generally more favourable evidence than tone: the same 1005-patient study reported sensitivity 60%, specificity 68% for perianal sensation; an older meta-analysis by Fairbank et al. reported high specificity for perianal sensory loss. The heterogeneity itself is the honest teaching point here, but this is not the same evidence base as anal tone and shouldn't be dismissed alongside it |

!!! info "A normal panel together still carries some weight"
    Testing tone in isolation performs poorly, but there is some evidence that a fully normal panel, tone, perianal sensation, anal squeeze and bulbocavernosus reflex all together, carries more reassurance than any single component alone. This is a different claim from "anal tone is individually useful", and it's easy to blur the two without noticing, worth holding this loosely given how thin the underlying evidence for the combined-panel claim still is.
| Bulbocavernosus reflex (BCR) | One small study reported 100% sensitivity and 100% specificity, the best-performing single sign identified, but based on limited numbers and a test many non-specialists are unfamiliar with |
| Anal squeeze | One study reported sensitivity 29%, specificity 96% |

**Teaching point:** no single examination finding, including DRE, reliably rules CES in or out. A normal PR exam should not be used to defer imaging or discharge a patient with a genuinely concerning history. Despite this, DRE remains widely regarded as part of a comprehensive assessment and is commonly expected from a medicolegal perspective, this tension is picked up again in the [Medicolegal note](#medicolegal-note) below.

## Investigations

Emergency MRI is the investigation of choice and should not be delayed for a PR examination result if MRI access is the rate-limiting step. The GIRFT-recommended protocol is specific and worth getting right: T2-weighted **sagittal and axial lumbar spine** first, and only **if this is negative** for a cause of the patient's symptoms, add a T2-weighted **sagittal** (not axial) scan of the cervical and thoracic spine to look for a more rostral cause of cord or root compression. This staged approach, rather than a full whole-spine axial protocol from the outset, keeps scan time down to around 15 minutes rather than 30, which matters when MRI capacity is the bottleneck ([GIRFT-aligned QI project, 2025](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12121611/)).

!!! question "Controversy: can a bladder scan safely rule out CES?"
    Post-void residual (PVR) bladder scanning has been promoted as a more objective adjunct than physical examination, but how much weight to put on it is genuinely contested.

    - [Venkatesan et al., *Spine* 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31479434/): a PVR <200 mL carried a negative predictive value of around 97%, and gave a CES probability of only 3.6%; a PVR >200 mL raised the probability of CES to 43%.
    - [Katzouraki et al., *Bone & Joint Journal* 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32475252/): a prospective study of 260 patients found a PVR cut-off of ≥200 mL was significantly more accurate than clinical red flags for predicting cauda equina compression on subsequent MRI, sensitivity 94%, specificity 66.8%, negative predictive value 98.7%.
    - [Audit of 50 medicolegal CES cases, *International Orthopaedics* 2022](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC9117366/): more than half (54%) had a PVR of ≤200 mL despite confirmed CES, all classified as CESI, all proceeding to emergency decompression, directly challenging the safety of using a <200 mL PVR to delay or avoid emergency MRI.
    - **Working position:** PVR is a useful adjunct in a patient without other red flags, not a standalone test that can safely override clinical concern or a convincing history. **A normal PVR must not be used to override the GIRFT pathway or clinical suspicion, and should never on its own delay imaging in a patient who otherwise looks like CES.** This is worth stating as a direct take-home rather than something merely implied by the numbers above.

## Differential diagnosis

Conus medullaris syndrome, mechanical low back pain, peripheral neuropathy, and functional neurological disorder are the key differentials.

The last of these is a genuinely important and under-taught area. In the same prospective cohort referenced above ([Hoeritzauer et al., *Neurology* 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33177221/)), patients with "scan-negative" CES (normal MRI) had significantly more positive clinical signs of functional neurological disorder (68% vs 11% in scan-positive patients), and history or presence of functional symptoms, normal patellar reflexes, more severe pain, and panic attack at presentation were all associated with a scan-negative outcome. This doesn't change the emergency imaging pathway, a normal MRI is still required to exclude compression, but it's worth knowing that a substantial proportion of the suspected-CES caseload will turn out to have no cauda equina compression at all, some of these patients will have a functional neurological disorder, and they still need honest, careful follow-up rather than being written off once compression is excluded.

## Management

Surgical decompression is indicated for CESI, CESR and CES-Complete **in radiographically confirmed CES**. Timing is only one operative consideration, the approach itself (typically posterior microdiscectomy or laminectomy, depending on the level and pathology causing compression) is chosen for whichever route achieves the most effective decompression of the compressed roots, not dictated by timing alone. The [GIRFT pathway](https://gettingitrightfirsttime.co.uk/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/National-Suspected-Cauda-Equina-Pathway-March-2026.pdf) classifies CESI surgery as an NCEPOD E1/E2 emergency, time-sensitive and life-changing though not immediately life-threatening, and states any reason for delay should be documented. Timing of surgery for CESR is explicitly left to the operating surgeon's discretion, but the pathway notes that even though CESR carries a poorer prognosis, around 70% of these patients will still benefit from decompression, worth knowing since it's easy to assume a late presentation isn't worth operating on.

!!! question "Controversy: does timing within 24-48h actually change outcome?"
    This is a good exam topic precisely because it resists a clean answer, and because two groups of authors have published a direct, on-the-record disagreement about it.

    - A prospective cohort of 33 patients ([Qureshi & Sell, *European Spine Journal* 2007](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17828560/)) found no significant difference in outcome between <24h, 24-48h, and >48h groups. Continence status at presentation, not time-to-surgery, was the dominant prognostic factor. The authors argued this challenged assumptions embedded in "surgical and medico-legal thinking."
    - This was directly challenged in a [published letter to the editor by Todd (2009), with the authors' reply](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2899530/), drawing on his own earlier work arguing timing does matter. The disagreement was never resolved in the literature.
    - A pooled systematic-review logistic regression of 322 patients found sensory/motor deficit, urinary incontinence and rectal dysfunction more likely to improve with decompression within 48h, though the review is explicit that timing definitions vary so much between studies that pooling them is itself methodologically shaky.
    - The [systematic review by Mustafa et al. (2023)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10159340/) found only 14.5% of 110 studies used an established CES definition at all, and timing definitions were similarly inconsistent, part of why this question remains genuinely unsettled rather than just old.

    **Working position, worth remembering above everything else on this page:**

    - Decompress as soon as feasible rather than promising patients a specific hour-count.
    - Continence status at presentation is the most consistently reported prognostic factor across current observational studies, more so than time to surgery.
    - Document clinical reasoning explicitly, given how disproportionately this condition features in litigation.

## Complications

- **Persistent bladder/bowel/sexual dysfunction**, more likely with longer-standing or more severe compression at presentation.
- **Failure to improve despite timely decompression**, a recognised and difficult outcome, worth setting as a realistic expectation rather than assuming timely surgery guarantees recovery.

## Prognosis

In the largest UK prospective cohort ([Woodfield et al., 621 participants](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9678980/)), catheterisation for urinary retention was required pre-operatively in 31% of patients; by discharge this had fallen to 13%, indicating meaningful but incomplete recovery is the typical postoperative course, not full and immediate resolution. Continence status at presentation remains the single most consistently reported prognostic factor across current observational studies, more so than time to surgery, though it's worth noting the underlying evidence base is observational rather than high-quality controlled data.

## Medicolegal note

CES is disproportionately represented in NHS litigation. [NHS Resolution](https://resolution.nhs.uk/resources/cauda-equina-syndrome/) received 827 claims relating to CES between January 2008 and December 2018, of which 340 were settled with damages at a total cost of £186,134,049. The [GIRFT pathway](https://gettingitrightfirsttime.co.uk/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/National-Suspected-Cauda-Equina-Pathway-March-2026.pdf) itself states that 23% of all litigated claims for spinal surgery in England relate to CES. Delays in recognition in primary care and delays in MRI access, rather than surgical delay once CES is diagnosed, are the most commonly cited contributing factors.

This is where the DRE tension from the examination section above actually matters in practice: a normal anal tone finding has weak diagnostic value, but omitting DRE entirely from documentation is itself viewed unfavourably in litigation, since it looks like an incomplete assessment regardless of what the finding would have shown. The pragmatic position is to examine and document thoroughly, including DRE, while not treating a reassuring DRE as a reason to avoid or delay imaging.

There is early evidence the GIRFT pathway itself is improving care: an independent study from the Northern Care Alliance, published in *The Neuroradiology Journal*, found that after implementing GIRFT guidance, hospital admissions for suspected CES fell from 57% to 43% of cases, with same-day scanning rates rising from 53% to 62%, and no change in the proportion of scans that actually identified CES (4% in both periods), meaning the improvement in efficiency didn't come at the cost of missed diagnoses ([Thiagarajah et al.](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12507797/)).
