# Spinal Epidural Abscess

## Introduction

Spinal epidural abscess (SEA) is a suppurative infection within the spinal epidural space causing cord or cauda equina compromise through a combination of direct mechanical compression, thrombophlebitis, and vascular compromise. It is rare, roughly 0.2-2 cases per 10,000 hospital admissions in the largest meta-analysis of the condition, but the incidence has been rising, attributed to an ageing population with more comorbidity, rising rates of intravenous drug use, and increasing numbers of spinal injections and instrumented procedures.

## Aetiology and risk factors

*Staphylococcus aureus* accounts for the large majority of cases. Spread is most commonly haematogenous (from a distant focus such as skin or soft tissue infection, urinary tract infection, or an infected line), but contiguous spread from adjacent discitis/osteomyelitis and direct inoculation (epidural injection, spinal surgery, epidural catheter) both occur.

Recognised risk factors: diabetes mellitus, intravenous drug use, immunosuppression, chronic renal or hepatic disease, indwelling vascular catheters, and any recent spinal intervention. In one case-control study, one or more of these risk factors was present in 98% of confirmed SEA patients versus 21% of controls, arguably a more useful screening question in a patient with new back pain than waiting for the classic triad below.

## Natural history: Heusner's staging

Heusner's 1948 description of the natural history remains the standard teaching framework, a four-stage progression:

1. Back pain at the level of the abscess
2. Nerve root pain radiating from that level
3. Motor weakness, sensory deficit, and bladder/bowel dysfunction
4. Paralysis

Progression through these stages can take hours in fulminant cases or evolve over days to weeks in more indolent presentations, this variability is part of why the diagnosis is so often missed early.

## Clinical presentation, and the triad myth

The "classic triad" of back pain, fever, and neurological deficit is taught almost universally as the way to recognise SEA. It is a genuinely poor screening tool, and this is worth knowing explicitly rather than assuming the textbook description reflects how patients actually present.

- A landmark meta-analysis of 915 patients established the figures most subsequent reviews still cite: the full triad has a sensitivity of only around 7.9%, though when it is present, it is highly specific (99.2%) for SEA.
- More recent cohorts confirm this hasn't changed: a study of 457 patients found the classic triad present in only 10% at diagnosis, and an emergency department case-control study found it in just 13% of confirmed cases (versus 1% of controls with similar presentations who did not have SEA).
- Back pain is by far the most consistent individual feature, present in roughly two-thirds to 90% of cases depending on the series. Fever is present in a minority to around two-thirds of cases across different cohorts, and is not a reliable rule-out on its own.

**Teaching point:** don't wait for all three features of the triad before considering SEA. A patient with new or worsening back pain and any recognised risk factor deserves the diagnosis actively considered, and inflammatory markers checked, well before fever or a neurological deficit appears. Diagnostic delay is consistently reported as the leading driver of poor outcomes and of medicolegal claims relating to SEA, this is picked up again below.

## Investigations

- **Gadolinium-enhanced MRI of the whole spine** is the investigation of choice and the only modality that reliably defines the extent of the abscess and degree of cord compression. Non-contrast MRI can miss or underestimate an abscess, worth being specific that gadolinium is required when requesting the scan.
- **Inflammatory markers** (ESR and CRP) are elevated in the large majority of confirmed cases (ESR above 20mm/h in around 94% of cases in one series) and are far more sensitive than a raised white cell count, though neither is specific to SEA. Useful as a triage/screening adjunct in a patient with risk factors and pain, not as a diagnostic test in isolation.
- **Blood cultures**, positive in a large majority of cases given how commonly SEA arises from bacteraemia, should be sent before starting empirical antibiotics wherever the patient's clinical state allows.
- **Image-guided or intra-operative biopsy** for organism identification where blood cultures are negative or the diagnosis is otherwise unclear, important for targeting the prolonged antibiotic course that follows.

## Differential diagnosis

Discitis/vertebral osteomyelitis without epidural extension, mechanical back pain, metastatic spinal cord compression, transverse myelitis, and other causes of acute cord or cauda equina compression.

## Management

!!! question "Controversy: antibiotics alone, or decompression?"
    This mirrors the CES and bilateral facet dislocation timing debates in structure, if not in specifics: the overall evidence doesn't clearly favour one default strategy, but specific patient factors change the answer considerably.

    - The largest systematic review and meta-analysis to date (808 patients, 20 studies) found **no statistically significant difference in neurological outcome** between surgical and conservative management overall. However, **delayed surgery was significantly associated with a worse outcome**, an important nuance: the finding isn't "surgery doesn't matter," it's "if surgery is going to happen, doing it promptly matters a great deal."
    - Predictors consistently associated with failure of antibiotics-alone management include: significant or progressive neurological deficit at presentation, MRSA as the causative organism, age over 65, diabetes, markedly elevated CRP, and leukocytosis. One larger series specifically identified motor deficit at presentation, a pathological or compression fracture at the affected level, active malignancy, diabetes, sensory change, and a dorsal (posterior) location of the abscess as independent predictors of failure.
    - In practice, a substantial proportion of patients started on antibiotics alone will still end up needing surgery, one series reported 41% of conservatively-managed patients required delayed surgical intervention.
    - **Working position:** a neurologically intact patient without the risk factors above can reasonably be trialled on targeted antibiotics with very close neurological monitoring and a low threshold for repeat MRI, but any neurological deficit at presentation, or any deterioration during a trial of antibiotics, is an indication for urgent surgical decompression. This isn't a philosophical choice between two equally valid strategies, it's active risk-stratification of an individual patient.

**Antibiotic therapy**: empirical broad-spectrum cover (typically including MRSA cover pending sensitivities) is started promptly once cultures are secured, then rationalised to the causative organism once known. Courses are prolonged, commonly of the order of six to eight weeks of targeted therapy, guided by microbiology/infectious diseases input and clinical/inflammatory marker response rather than a fixed duration.

## Prognosis

Prognosis correlates directly with pre-treatment neurological status and the duration of any deficit before decompression, full neurological recovery is substantially less likely once paralysis has been present for more than 24 hours, one of the strongest arguments for urgent recognition and action rather than a specific hour-based cut-off. Outcomes are worse in the elderly, in diabetics, and in MRSA infection.

| [Reihsaus E et al., Spinal epidural abscess: a meta-analysis of 915 patients, *Neurosurgical Review* 2000](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11153548/) | The foundational meta-analysis. Classic triad sensitivity 7.9%, specificity 99.2%. Diabetes the most common risk factor. |
| Heusner AP, Nontuberculous spinal epidural infections, *New England Journal of Medicine* 1948 | Original description of the four-stage natural history (back pain, radicular pain, weakness, paralysis) still used as the standard teaching framework. |
| [Davis DP et al., The clinical presentation and impact of diagnostic delays on emergency department patients with spinal epidural abscess](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15028325/) | Case-control study. | [Diagnostic Delays Are Common, and Classic Presentations Are Rare in Spinal Epidural Abscess, 2024](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC12208084/) | 457 patients. Classic triad present in only 10%. Over two-thirds had at least one prior healthcare visit before diagnosis. |
| [Wang TY et al., Neurological Outcomes After Surgical or Conservative Management of Spontaneous Spinal Epidural Abscesses, *Clinical Spine Surgery* 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30589647/) | Systematic review and meta-analysis, 808 patients, 20 studies. No significant difference in outcome between surgical and conservative management overall; delayed surgery specifically associated with worse outcome. |
