    Bilateral Facet Dislocation¶

    Curriculum mapping

    T&O curriculum: Trauma module, Specific fracture areas: Spine, "The acute fracture and dislocation" (p.60, level 2/4/4) and "Spinal shock and cord syndromes" (p.60, level 3/4/4).

    Skills: Trauma Spine, Cervical, anterior/posterior fixation for fracture/dislocation, application of halo/tong traction, MUA fracture/dislocation (p.66, level 0/1/4).

    A classic FRCS (Tr & Orth) viva topic: it combines an acute decision-making scenario, a genuine unresolved evidence controversy (pre-reduction MRI), and a named classification system, exactly the combination examiners favour.

    See also: Cervical Spine Trauma, Spinal Cord Injury, and Cauda Equina Syndrome for the equivalent timing-of-decompression debate in a different part of the spine.

    Introduction¶

    Bilateral facet dislocation (BFD) is a high-energy flexion-distraction injury of the subaxial cervical spine in which both inferior articular processes of the upper vertebra displace anteriorly past the superior articular processes of the vertebra below, "jumping" or "locking" the facets. It represents complete disruption of the posterior ligamentous complex, the facet capsules, and typically the posterior longitudinal ligament and disc, making it one of the most unstable injuries in the subaxial cervical spine. Neurological injury, ranging from root symptoms to complete tetraplegia, is common given the degree of canal compromise at the moment of injury.

    Mechanism and associated injury¶

    Distraction combined with flexion is the classic mechanism, most often from road traffic collisions, diving into shallow water, or falls. Because bilateral facet dislocation requires disruption of the entire posterior tension band plus the disco-ligamentous complex, a concurrent traumatic disc herniation is common, this is the anatomical basis for the central management controversy below.

    Classification¶

    Distinguish bilateral (both facet joints dislocated at one level) from unilateral facet dislocation (a rotational injury, usually less unstable, with a lower rate of complete cord injury). Within bilateral injuries, facets may be perched (articular surfaces resting on top of one another, partial dislocation) or locked/jumped (fully displaced, articular surfaces facing each other, mechanically more stable in the displaced position and typically harder to reduce closed).

    The Subaxial Cervical Spine Injury Classification (SLIC) score, developed by the Spine Trauma Study Group, is the most widely used system for subaxial injuries generally and is worth being fluent in in this context:

    Category Finding Points
    Morphology No abnormality 0
    Compression 1
    Burst 2
    Distraction (facet perch, hyperextension) 3
    Rotation/translation (facet dislocation) 4
    Disco-ligamentous complex Intact 0
    Indeterminate 1
    Disrupted 2
    Neurological status Intact 0
    Root injury 1
    Complete cord injury 2
    Incomplete cord injury 3
    Ongoing cord compression in a patient with a neurological deficit +1

    A total score of 4 or less generally favours non-operative management, 4 is indeterminate, and 5 or more favours surgery. A bilateral facet dislocation with any neurological deficit will typically already score well into the surgical range from morphology and neurology alone. Worth knowing for the viva: SLIC's inter-rater reliability for the disco-ligamentous complex component specifically was only moderate in the original validation study, which is part of why the AO Spine subaxial classification has since been developed as a more hierarchical, better-validated successor, useful if an examiner pushes on the limitations of SLIC rather than just its content.

    Clinical presentation¶

    Neck pain and deformity following high-energy trauma, with a wide spectrum of neurological findings from normal through radiculopathy to complete tetraplegia below the injury level. Assess and document neurological status thoroughly and immediately on arrival, this baseline matters enormously both for management decisions and later medicolegal scrutiny.

    Investigations¶

    CT is the investigation of choice for characterising the bony injury and facet position. MRI defines the disco-ligamentous complex, any traumatic disc herniation, and the degree of cord compression or intramedullary signal change, but when to obtain it relative to reduction is the central controversy in this topic.

    Controversy: is pre-reduction MRI necessary before closed reduction?

    This is one of the longest-running unresolved debates in spine trauma, and it centres on one landmark study whose own lead author later described the safety question as still "unclear."

    • Vaccaro et al., Spine 1999: a prospective study of 11 awake, neurologically intact or incomplete patients undergoing awake closed traction reduction, with MRI both before and after. Disc herniation was identified in several patients before reduction, yet none deteriorated neurologically after reduction, including patients whose disc herniations were not addressed surgically first. This became the primary evidence cited for proceeding to closed reduction without routine pre-reduction MRI in an awake, cooperative, examinable patient.
    • Multiple subsequent case reports describe patients who deteriorated neurologically after closed reduction performed without pre-reduction imaging, attributed to retropulsion of herniated disc material into the canal during the reduction manoeuvre, the scenario pre-reduction MRI is specifically intended to prevent.
    • A survey of the Spine Trauma Study Group's own members found highly variable practice regarding both the timing and influence of MRI on management, confirming this remains a genuine, unresolved practice variation among specialists, not merely a historical debate that's since been settled.
    • Working position, and the actual examinable distinction: awake closed reduction in a cooperative, examinable, neurologically intact or incomplete patient, with the reduction attempted under direct neurological monitoring and abandoned immediately if the patient deteriorates, is supported by the original evidence base without routine pre-reduction MRI. Patients who are obtunded, intubated, intoxicated, or otherwise not neurologically examinable during the procedure cannot provide this safety monitoring, and for this group pre-reduction MRI to exclude a significant disc herniation is the more widely supported approach. The distinction is examinability during the reduction, not just "is a herniation present."

    Management¶

    Closed reduction in the awake, examinable patient is typically attempted first: serial cervical traction using a halo ring or Gardner-Wells tongs, with weight added incrementally (historical series describe safely using up to 140 lb in a graded fashion under close neurological monitoring) while repeating neurological examination between increments and stopping immediately if any deterioration occurs. Sedation is used cautiously, since it can mask the neurological monitoring the whole technique depends on.

    Open reduction is indicated when closed reduction fails, when the patient cannot be safely examined during the attempt, or when a significant disc herniation has been identified and needs addressing before or during reduction. An anterior approach is classically favoured when there is a concurrent disc herniation requiring discectomy, though a combined anterior-posterior approach is often needed given the degree of posterior ligamentous disruption typically present in a genuinely bilateral, locked injury.

    Controversy: does timing of decompression change neurological outcome here?

    This mirrors the timing debate on the Cauda Equina Syndrome page closely enough that it's worth drawing the parallel explicitly for viva purposes, complete with its own published disagreement between authors.

    • STASCIS, Fehlings et al., PLoS One 2012: the largest prospective multicentre study on this question, comparing early (<24h) versus late (≥24h) decompression after traumatic cervical spinal cord injury. The unadjusted analysis found a significantly higher proportion of patients achieving at least a 2-grade AIS improvement at 6 months in the early group.
    • This finding was directly challenged in a published letter to the editor by van Middendorp, who reanalysed the raw data presented in the paper and reached a different conclusion about the statistical strength of the association, another example, like the CES timing literature, of a headline "time matters" finding being contested on methodological grounds by another specialist rather than being straightforwardly settled.
    • A subsequent AO Spine clinical practice guideline (2017) nonetheless recommends decompression within 24 hours where feasible for acute traumatic cervical spinal cord injury, reflecting that the balance of evidence, imperfect as it is, still favours earlier surgery.
    • Working position: aim for decompression, whether by successful closed reduction or surgery, within 24 hours where the patient's overall condition allows, while recognising the underlying trial evidence for this specific cut-off has been directly disputed by at least one specialist reanalysis, useful nuance for a viva answer that wants to show awareness beyond "time is spine."

    Complications¶

    • Neurological deterioration during reduction, the central risk this whole topic is organised around.
    • Vertebral artery injury, worth considering with any cervical facet injury, particularly with a rotational/translational component.
    • Failure of closed reduction, more likely with locked/jumped facets than perched facets, and more likely the longer reduction is delayed from injury.
    • Construct failure or non-union after stabilisation, relevant to the anterior-only versus combined approach decision above.

    Study Key finding

Disc herniation present in several patients did not preclude safe awake closed reduction under neurological monitoring.
